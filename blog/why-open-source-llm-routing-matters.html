Why Open-Source LLM Routing Matters

March 4, 2026

There are a handful of LLM routing products on the market now. Most of them work the same way: you send your API traffic through their servers, their classifier picks a model, and you get a response back. You pay a fee on top of the LLM cost. The routing logic itself? Closed source, running on their infrastructure, making decisions about your prompts that you have no way to verify.

We think that's the wrong model for something this critical.

What actually goes wrong with proprietary routers

The pitch sounds great: "just point your base URL at us and save money." But in practice, three things tend to happen.

First, the classifier makes bad decisions for your specific workload and you have no recourse. Every routing product trains or tunes its classifier on some dataset. If your prompts look different from that training data, routing quality degrades. With a closed-source router, your only option is to file a support ticket and hope they adjust something on their end. You cannot look at the classification logic, you cannot add a rule for your edge case, and you cannot retrain the model on your own data.

Second, your prompts flow through someone else's servers. For teams working on proprietary code, healthcare data, financial systems, or anything covered by an NDA, this is a non-starter. Some routers claim they don't log prompts. You have no way to verify that claim.

Third, pricing changes without warning. We have seen routing services introduce per-request fees, change their margin structure, or add usage tiers after teams are already integrated. Once your production stack depends on a third-party proxy, switching costs are real.

What open-source routing gives you

Nadir is MIT-licensed. The entire codebase, including the complexity classifier, the routing logic, and the proxy server, is on GitHub. Here is what that means in practice.

You can read the classifier. The heuristic classifier is a single Python module. When a prompt gets misrouted, you open the file, find the rule that fired, and understand exactly why. Try doing that with a proprietary API.

You can change the classifier. If your workload has a specific pattern (say, lots of SQL generation prompts that the default classifier underestimates), you can add a rule or adjust thresholds. Fork the repo, make the change, done. The hosted version of Nadir also supports trained ML classifiers if you want something more sophisticated, but the point is that you have the option.

Your prompts stay on your machine. Nadir runs as a local proxy on localhost:8856. Your requests go directly from Nadir to the LLM provider (OpenAI, Anthropic, Google). No intermediate servers, no third-party logging, no data residency questions.

You control the model lineup. New model drops from Anthropic? Add it to your config and set the tier mapping. A provider has a 4-hour outage? Nadir's health monitor detects the failures and routes around it automatically. You do not wait for a vendor to push an update.

The case for community-owned infrastructure

LLM routing is infrastructure. It sits in the critical path of every API call your application makes. The argument for open-sourcing infrastructure is well-established: Linux, Kubernetes, PostgreSQL, Redis, Nginx. The software that everything else depends on should be inspectable, modifiable, and not controlled by a single company.

LLM costs affect every developer building with AI. The routing layer that reduces those costs should not be another SaaS dependency with opaque pricing and closed logic. It should be something the community can audit, improve, and own.

That's why Nadir's core router is MIT-licensed with no usage restrictions. Use it commercially, fork it, embed it in your product. The code is yours.

Open source does not mean unsupported

We also offer a hosted version at getnadir.com with a trained ML classifier, analytics dashboard, Stripe billing integration, and managed infrastructure. If you want the convenience of a hosted service with the transparency of open source, that option exists. But the self-hosted open-source version is fully functional on its own. It is not a crippled free tier designed to push you toward a paid plan.
